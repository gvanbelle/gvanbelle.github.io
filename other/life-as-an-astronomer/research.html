Research Interests

There are two specific areas to this page: a research statement, discussing my interests in astronomy; and postscript versions of my papers, documenting that research.


Research Statement

Specifically qualifiing my research interests in astronomy, my primary interest is in experimental astrophysics, specializing in interferometry. I have a great deal of experience in observational systems, from the UV to the visible to the IR, from ground-based to space-borne observatories. In a nutshell, I know how to make things work, from design to construction to operations to troubleshooting; machining, optics, electronics, programming, and even plumbing are skills I've had to master along the road. Looking at 'the road' itself can fill in the blanks:

When I originally started at Whitman College back in 1986, I was interested in the 3-2 engineering program that the college had with Caltech.  However, even though I was one of the few accepted to the program (5 from roughly 150), I had been bitten by the astronomy bug and decided to stay at Whitman, finishing out my degree in Physics-Astronomy, minoring in math (in contrast to going onto to Caltech and doing double-E).

From there I went on to The Johns Hopkins University. While concentrating on the usual curriculum of graduate level physics, I never strayed far from my original roots in engneering while pursuing my interest in astronomy. Among my more notable activities at JHU were the installation and operation of a 20" DFM telescope on the roof of the new physics building, in addition to working with the Sounding Rocket Group on a ultraviolet spectrophotometer payload. The latter endeavor had me spending considerable time at Wallops Flight Facility with flight qualification activities.

Finishing out a master's degree at JHU, I moved onto the University of Wyoming, where I worked with H. Mel Dyck and Bob Thompson on the Infrared Optical Telescope Array (IOTA) interferometer. Given the capabilities of this instrument, we concentrated on stellar astrophysics, primarily late stages of stellar evolution - giant stars, supergiants, carbon stars, Mira variables. Angular diameters, and subsequently linear radii and effective temperatures, of these stars were the main thrust of the research; surface brightness distributions for these objects were also an area of active research. I also spent considerable amount of time at Wyoming Infrared Observatory (WIRO), the department's 2.3 m telescope on nearby Mt. Jelm. Between IOTA and WIRO, I spent about 1/4 of my total time at observatory sites (90 days out of the year).

I am now working at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, continuing to collaborate with Mel and Bob on the research I started in Wyoming. Nowadays there's much less research time in my schedule; however, it now includes time up at the Palomar Testbed Interferometer. In addition to research, I spend the majority of my time working on the design of the Keck Interferometer.
